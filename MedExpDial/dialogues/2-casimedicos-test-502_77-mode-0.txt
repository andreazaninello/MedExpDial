Bronchiolitis is indeed the most common cause of hospitalization in infants under 12 months, especially in the fall and winter months. This condition is usually caused by Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV). 

Regarding the recommended tests, as a general rule in pediatrics, each proposed exam must be rational and absolutely necessary since each procedure can be a source of stress and must be fully justified. Considering our case, the child is stable and is in moderate respiratory distress, not severe. And as you rightly stated, bronchiolitis, which is clinically probable, is mostly of viral origin and the diagnosis is mainly clinical.

1. Chest X-Ray: In infants with moderate to severe disease, it may show hyperinflation and peribronchial cuffing, though these findings are not specific. Chest radiography could show pneumonia, but it does not show bronchiolitis, so it is not useful in most cases because the results do not change the treatment.

2. Blood tests (CBC) : Blood tests are not routinely recommended. They cause unnecessary pain, and results are not likely to influence decision-making for typical cases of bronchiolitis. 

3. Nasopharyngeal swab for a viral panel: This test could identify the virus responsible for bronchiolitis, but it will not change the clinical management. It is usually reserved for specific situations (severe illness, hospitalization, investigational purposes)

Blood gas changes, either arterial or capillary, are not necessary as routine monitoring tools in infants and children with bronchiolitis. They are usually reserved for severe disease.

To conclude, the diagnosis of bronchiolitis is clinical, and routine tests are not recommended in all infants and children with the disease. So, the most appropriate attitude at this time would be to continue clinical monitoring as respiratory status can evolve, and providing symptomatic treatment (hydration, oxygen if needed).
