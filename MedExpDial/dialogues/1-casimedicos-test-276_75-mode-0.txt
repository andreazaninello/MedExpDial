STUDENT: Based on the given serologic profile, it appears that this patient has a chronic Hepatitis B Virus (HBV) infection. More specifically, this indicates a state of inactive, carrier status.

Here's how we interpret each of those individual serologic data points:

- Hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) positivity suggests that the patient is infected with the HBV as this antigen is a part of the virus. Absence of Anti-HBs (antibody against HBsAg) further supports this.
  
- The Hepatitis B "e" antigen (HBeAg) is a marker for active viral replication. If positive, this suggests that the person is highly infectious. A minus sign here shows that the viral replication is low, but that may not always be the case. Anti-HBe antibodies suggest that active viral replication may have decreased and the course of infection may be resolving.

- HBc or Hepatitis B core antibody comes in two types; IgM and IgG. IgM anti-HBc tends to appear early in the infection and disappears typically within 6 months, indicating a recent infection. Therefore, a negative result implies that the infection was not recent or the patient is in the window period (i.e. the period between the disappearance of HBsAg and appearance of Anti-HBs). IgG anti-HBc typically appears shortly after HBsAg is detected and can be present for life indicating a chronic or past infection. In this case, the positive IgG anti-HBc supports the likelihood of a chronic infection.

- Furthermore, the detection of HBV DNA demonstrates that the virus is present, and thus replicating in the patient.

Of note, one must remember that the child likely acquired the infection from perinatal transmission (which is common in endemic areas like Nigeria), as most newly acquired HBV infections in children that age are asymptomatic and go onto develop chronic hepatitis.
